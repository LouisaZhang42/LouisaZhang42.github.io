üìàBackground

As autonomous vehicle technology advances into the L2-L3 phase, challenges related to distracted driving in multi-task scenarios are also emerging. Recent studies [ 1, 2, 3, 4, 5] have highlighted the impact of increasingly complex in-vehicle information systems on drivers' distraction during take-over. As such, it's important to redesign the imformation system to ensure effective autonomous driving.


 

 


üìåProblem Define


Failure to allocate sufficient cognitive resources for safe driving.

Cognitive distractions play the most important role among cognitive, visual, and manual
distractions [6]. Since the skills required for L2-L3 drivers are still under discussion,
I mainly consider the scenarios where the driver has taken over.


üåü As the field of "cognitive resourses" belongs to Cognitive Psychology, while the general user research paradigm is based on Sociology and Anthropology, improving the scientific accuracy of the findings requires an interdisciplinary approach. To this end, my tutor advised me to refer to the Explanatory Design [7] and integrate top-down [8] psychological research methods with bottom-up [8] user research methods. Specifically, my research would consist of the following three steps:

- I. Search the literature for theoretical analysis and review of the problem.
- ii. Apply previous theories to provide an interdisciplinary explanation of the problem and propose hypothetical design strategies
- iii. Test the assumptions through user research and develop deeper user insights.
Following these steps, I embarked on my formal research.

 

üìñTheory & Strategy
Problem Analysis

According to the information processing model [9] of Cognitive Psychology, I marked the cognitive issues that drivers would encounter when processing the environmental information inputs.


Core Problems:
¬∑ Inefficient messaging within the driving system.
¬∑ Improper allocation of attention.

Secondary Problems:
¬∑ Reduced cognitive resources for driving: negative emotions and fatigue [ 3, 10]

 

‚öóÔ∏èHypotheses

I was inspired by the idea of Connectionism [11] and and neural network. I took the driver's cognitive resources allocated to each task as my design outputs. To clarify how to manipulate the hidden layer, I introduce Information Theory [12] and Cybernetic [ 13, 14 ] models to control the information transmission in the driving system. The features of attention [ 15, 16, 17] were also used to regulate attention resources in each channel. After completing the strategy modeling, I proposed 7 design hypotheses.

¬∑Shannon's Information Theory model can be extended to human information processing.
¬∑Cybernetic explores how to regulate the structure, constraint, and possibilities of systems with an interdisciplinary approach.

üìëUser Experiment
Focus Group

To test my 7 design hypotheses, I translated them into 12 functional descriptions. Then I conducted the experiment in two focus groups (N=12) and guided the users to evaluate the functions. The focus groups were conducted with a semi-structured outline. Males and females were assigned to different groups to avoid culture conflicts.
